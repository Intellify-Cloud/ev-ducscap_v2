## Why Interest Rates Matter

A home loan is one of the largest financial commitments most South
Africans will ever make. Even a small change in interest rates can
significantly affect your monthly repayment and the total amount you
repay over the life of your bond. For example, a 0.25% increase may only
add a few hundred rand to your monthly instalment, but over a 20 or 30
year loan, that difference can amount to tens of thousands of rand in
additional interest. The opposite is also true. When interest rates
fall, your monthly repayments become more affordable, leaving you with
more disposable income or the opportunity to pay extra into your bond.
\## Understanding the Repo Rate and Prime Lending Rate To understand why
your bond repayments change, it helps to know how interest rates are
set. The South African Reserve Bank (SARB) determines the repo rate,
which is the rate at which commercial banks borrow money from the
central bank. The Monetary Policy Committee reviews this rate regularly,
taking into account factors such as inflation, economic growth and
financial stability. Commercial banks then use the repo rate as the
foundation for their prime lending rate, adding a margin before lending
money to consumers and businesses. Your personal home loan interest rate
is based on the prime lending rate, adjusted according to your
individual risk profile. In simple terms: Repo Rate → Prime Lending Rate
→ Your Home Loan Interest Rate → Your Monthly Bond Repayment This is why
changes announced by the Reserve Bank eventually affect homeowners
across South Africa. \## Why Your Interest Rate May Differ from Someone
Else's Many buyers assume everyone receives the same home loan interest
rate. In reality, banks assess every application individually. Several
factors influence the rate you're offered, including: Your credit score
and repayment history The size of your deposit Your monthly income and
existing debt The value of the property The amount you're borrowing Your
overall financial risk Applicants with strong credit profiles and larger
deposits often qualify for lower interest rates than higher risk
borrowers. Working with a bond originator can also improve your chances
of securing a more competitive rate because multiple lenders can be
approached on your behalf. \## What Happens When Interest Rates
Increase? When the Reserve Bank raises the repo rate, banks generally
increase their prime lending rate. If you have a variable interest rate
home loan, your monthly bond repayment usually increases as well. Higher
repayments can affect your household budget in several ways: Less
disposable income each month Reduced borrowing power for prospective
buyers More expensive home loans Slower property market activity as
affordability declines This doesn't necessarily mean it's a bad time to
buy property. In many cases, higher interest rates reduce buyer
competition, giving motivated buyers greater negotiating power when
purchasing a home. \## What Happens When Interest Rates Decrease?
Interest rate cuts generally have the opposite effect. Lower interest
rates reduce monthly bond repayments for homeowners with variable rate
loans, making home ownership more affordable. They can also: Improve
affordability for first time buyers Increase the amount buyers qualify
to borrow Encourage property market activity Free up additional monthly
cash flow Many homeowners continue paying their previous repayment
amount even after a rate cut. This simple strategy allows more of each
payment to reduce the capital balance instead of interest, helping pay
off the bond sooner and reducing total interest over the loan term. \##
How Much Difference Can a Small Interest Rate Change Make? Many people
underestimate how much even a small adjustment can affect a home loan.
Consider a homeowner with a R2 million bond over 20 years. A relatively
small increase in interest rates can add hundreds of rand to the monthly
repayment. While this may seem manageable, that additional cost
continues every month until interest rates change again or the loan
balance reduces. Likewise, a decrease in interest rates can produce
meaningful monthly savings. Depending on the loan amount, homeowners
could save thousands of rand each year. Leading South African home loan
providers regularly demonstrate how rate movements can significantly
affect monthly repayments across different loan values. \## Should You
Wait for Interest Rates to Drop Before Buying? This is one of the most
common questions asked by prospective buyers. The answer depends on your
personal financial circumstances rather than trying to predict future
interest rate movements. Waiting for lower interest rates may improve
affordability, but property prices may increase while you wait. At the
same time, lower rates often attract more buyers, creating greater
competition for quality properties. Rather than attempting to time the
market perfectly, it's generally better to focus on your own financial
readiness. Ask yourself: Can I comfortably afford the repayments? Do I
have a stable income? Have I saved an adequate deposit? Is my credit
profile in good shape? If the answer is yes, purchasing a property that
suits your long term needs may be more beneficial than waiting for the
next interest rate announcement. \## How to Reduce the Impact of Higher
Interest Rates Although nobody can control interest rate decisions,
there are practical steps you can take to minimise their impact.
Building a larger deposit reduces the amount you need to borrow and may
help you qualify for a better interest rate. Maintaining a healthy
credit score demonstrates responsible financial behaviour and improves
your negotiating position with lenders. Paying more than the required
monthly instalment whenever possible reduces the outstanding loan
balance faster, resulting in less interest paid over the life of the
bond. Finally, comparing offers from multiple banks rather than
accepting the first approval can often lead to substantial savings over
time. \## Why Professional Guidance Matters Applying for a home loan is
